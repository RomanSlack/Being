How FlashAvatar Works

From a talking-head video to a real-time 3D avatar you can puppet with any expression — at 300+ FPS. A technical walkthrough of every step in the pipeline.

01 The Big Picture

The goal is simple: you have a video of yourself talking and you want to turn it into a 3D avatar that you can control in real time with any expression parameters.

This is fundamentally different from audio-driven approaches like GaussianTalker, where a neural network guesses what facial expression matches a given audio clip. That mapping is inherently lossy — the network can't know whether you raised your eyebrows or squinted while saying a word.

Key Insight
FlashAvatar's input is expression parameters, not audio. You feed it 120 numbers that describe a facial expression, and it renders exactly that expression. This means you can drive it with:
• Tracked expressions from another video (face reenactment)
• Game engine blend shapes
• Hand-crafted animation curves
• Any source that can produce FLAME parameters

The tradeoff: you need a separate system to produce those expression parameters (a tracker, a speech-to-expression model, etc.), but the rendering itself is a direct, lossless mapping from parameters → pixels.

02 FLAME: The Face Model

FLAME (Faces Learned with an Articulated Model and Expressions) is a parametric 3D face model. Think of it as a mathematical recipe for generating any human face shape and expression.

You give FLAME a set of numbers → it outputs a 3D mesh (a triangle soup of ~5,000 vertices that form a face). Different numbers = different face.

ParameterDimsWhat It Controls
shape300Your unique face geometry — bone structure, nose shape, jaw width, etc. This is your identity and stays constant.
expression100Muscle activations — smile, frown, raise eyebrows, puff cheeks, sneer, etc.
jaw6Jaw rotation — open mouth, move jaw left/right/forward.
eyes12Gaze direction for each eye (6 DoF per eye).
eyelids2How open/closed each eyelid is.

Total: ~120 controllable dimensions. These 120 numbers fully describe any facial expression a human can make.

Why This Is Powerful
If you can track these 120 numbers from a video — extracting them frame by frame — you can replay those expressions on a different face. You can mix and match: your identity + someone else's expressions. This is the foundation of face reenactment, and exactly what FlashAvatar exploits.

FLAME was trained on thousands of 3D face scans from real people, so its parameter space is well-behaved: interpolating between expressions produces natural results, and the shape space covers the full range of human facial geometry.

03 The Pipeline, Step by Step

Getting from a raw video to a puppetable avatar requires four stages. Each produces data that the next stage needs.

3a — MICA: Identity Extraction

Purpose
Figure out what your face looks like in FLAME's parameter space.

Input: A single photo of your face.

What it does: MICA is a neural network trained on thousands of 3D face scans paired with photos. It looks at your photo and predicts your FLAME shape parameters — the 300 numbers that define your unique face geometry. It's essentially answering: "what settings on the FLAME face-generator would produce this person's bone structure?"

Output: identity.npy — a 300-dimensional vector. This is "you" in FLAME space. It only needs to run once per person.

3b — Metrical-Tracker: Per-Frame FLAME Fitting

Bottleneck
This is the longest step — 4-6+ hours for a 7-minute video. Every other step takes minutes.

Input: Your video, cropped to 512×512 around the face. Plus the identity shape from MICA.

What it does for each frame:

  1. Detect facial landmarks — MediaPipe finds 478 2D points on the face (eye corners, lip edges, nose tip, jawline, etc.)
  2. Initialize from the previous frame's FLAME parameters (temporal coherence)
  3. Gradient descent optimization (~310 iterations across 4 resolution levels):
    • Render the current FLAME mesh estimate onto the image using differentiable rendering
    • Compute landmark loss: how far are the rendered mesh's landmarks from the detected 2D landmarks?
    • Compute photometric loss: how different does the rendered face look from the actual image pixel-by-pixel?
    • Update FLAME parameters (expression, jaw, eyes, eyelids) + camera pose to minimize total loss
    • Repeat at increasingly fine resolution (coarse → fine pyramid)
  4. Save a .frame checkpoint with: expression params, jaw pose, eye pose, eyelid params, camera intrinsics/extrinsics

Output: 10,080 .frame files — one per video frame — containing the complete FLAME parameter trajectory of your performance.

Why It's Slow
Each frame requires ~310 gradient steps of nonlinear optimization (render → compare → update → repeat). Frames are processed sequentially because each frame initializes from the previous one's result. GPU parallelism doesn't help — each step is a single 512×512 differentiable render. An RTX 3090 would be nearly the same speed as an A100 here.

3c — Masks: What the Avatar Should Learn

FlashAvatar doesn't just compare its rendering to the raw image — it uses masks to focus attention on the parts that matter and ignore the rest.

Original Frame512×512 cropped face — the input image that FlashAvatar learns to reproduce.
Alpha MaskFrom RobustVideoMatting. Grayscale — white = foreground (person), black = background. Smooth gradients at hair edges. Tells FlashAvatar: "only render pixels where the person is."
Neckhead MaskFrom BiSeNet face parsing. Binary mask of head + neck region. FlashAvatar computes perceptual loss (LPIPS) only within this region — "does my rendering look like the real image here?"
Mouth MaskFrom BiSeNet face parsing. Binary mask of mouth interior only. FlashAvatar applies 40× extra loss weight here — the mouth is the hardest part (teeth, tongue, dark interior), so it gets disproportionate training attention.

3d — FlashAvatar Training: Learning Your Appearance

The Fast Part
~5 minutes on an A100 GPU. 150,000 training iterations.

Input: Cropped images + masks + FLAME parameters from the tracker.

What it does:

  1. Initialize 3D Gaussian splats on the FLAME mesh surface — thousands of tiny colored blobs attached to the face mesh
  2. For each training iteration:
    • Pick a random training frame
    • Read that frame's FLAME parameters (expression, jaw, eyes, eyelids)
    • Deform the Gaussians according to those parameters — the Gaussians ride on the mesh surface and move with it
    • Render the deformed Gaussians to a 2D image using differentiable Gaussian splatting
    • Compare to real image: Huber loss + 40× mouth loss + perceptual loss (LPIPS, after 15K iters)
    • Update Gaussian properties (position offsets, color, opacity, size) + deformation network via backprop
  3. After 150K iterations: the Gaussians have learned your exact appearance — skin texture, hair, eyes, teeth, etc.

Output: A trained model (~50MB) that can render your face at any FLAME expression at 300+ FPS.

04 Why This Architecture?

The key insight: by attaching Gaussians to the FLAME mesh, you separate the problem into two parts that are each easy to solve:

FLAME handles geometry + deformation
Moving the mesh to a new expression is just plugging in new parameter values — no neural network inference needed. FLAME is a closed-form function.
Gaussians handle appearance
Each Gaussian stores color, opacity, and size. Training only needs to learn: "what does this person's skin/hair/eyes look like?" — not how faces move. That's already solved by FLAME.

This gives you three wins:

  1. Expression control for free — move the mesh → Gaussians follow → new expression rendered. The exact expression you specify is what you get.
  2. Real-time rendering — Gaussian splatting is massively parallel on GPU. Each Gaussian is independent, so the GPU can render all of them simultaneously. 300+ FPS.
  3. Fast training — only learning appearance (color/opacity per Gaussian), not geometry or deformation. 5 minutes vs. hours.

Indexing Gotcha
The tracker outputs 0-indexed checkpoints (00000.frame, 00001.frame, ...) while FlashAvatar expects 1-indexed images (00001.jpg, 00002.jpg, ...). FlashAvatar hardcodes frame_delta = 1 to bridge this gap: tracker frame 0 maps to image 1.
